INTERNSHIPS

Plug and Play Tech Centre - Singapore

September 30, 2019

Why Plug and Play

I got the opportunity to work at Plug and Play in Singapore due to the generosity of my friend Matt, who I met while studying at the Chinese Language Institute in Guilin. Back in 2017, we had been some of the only 外国人 (foreigners) in Guilin, and so despite him being a few years older than me, we had enjoyed exploring the town and the bars of Guilin together. After leaving Guilin, I returned to start my bachelor’s at Warwick, while Matt started working for Plug and Play in Singapore.

The great thing about Matt was that he was always willing to give me advice and act as a mentor: he had been through university and made his way in the corporate world; I was about to start and needed all the advice I could get. We kept in touch after leaving Guilin, reminiscing on the times in Guilin and discussing future plans. As summer approached, I realised that internship experience at Plug and Play, an innovative company at the interface between technology and investment, would help me figure out what kind of future career I wanted, and stand me in good stead for the future. I floated the idea of interning at Plug and Play, and Matt kindly forwarded my CV to the manager and put in a good word for me. Luckily the manager was in a good mood and gave me a shot. I was going to be interning at Plug and Play in Singapore!

My internship would be 6 weeks, starting in late August. This would not leave me with much time to relax at the end of summer, but it freed up 10 weeks for my brother and I to travel in Australia from June to August. The internship in Singapore would ‘break up’ the journey back from Oz.

My time in Singapore

Arrival in Singapore from Sydney was a relaxed affair for me. Not so much for my brother Peter, who needed to catch a connecting flight back to London. Peter and I had taken a fair few flights that summer, and the airlines seemed be making a habit of delaying our first connecting flight, and ensuring that the second one left bang on time. Suddenly a 2 hour layover was cut down to a 10 minute layover, and Peter was forced to blaze a trail across the airport to make his flight, with barely time to wave goodbye.

As he was boarding his flight, I sauntered out of the airport and took a taxi to the Airbnb I had rented to cover my time in Singapore. It was the middle of the night, but still hot and humid, as I lugged my bags up the dingy stairs into my room. I think it’s fair to say that I had chosen to live in one of the cheapest rooms in Singapore: a rickety thin bed, a wardrobe that fell apart every time it was opened, and a windowless room. It wasn’t the most pleasant place. I had chosen it in order to lessen the burden on my parents, who would be footing the bill. You see I had only been able to secure an unpaid internship, and so the whole time there was coming out of my (parents') pocket. Fair enough. The firm didn’t usually take interns as young as me, and there wasn’t much value a 19 year old could bring to the office in 6 weeks. The hope was that I would be reimbursed with a fun and useful experience.

After I had put all the bags down on the floor, I collapsed onto the bed, and slept…

The next morning I explored the local area a little, and bought some basic items that I would need while I was there. It was still a couple of days before I would start, so I had a look around some of Singapore, and made sure I knew how to make the commute to Centennial Tower, where I would be working.

The next day I met up with Matt and he showed me around the office before joining me for a pint to catch up.

For the remainder of my time in Singapore, I settled into a routine of working 9 to 5 at the office, and spending the evenings working out and chatting to friends from back home. On weekends I would be free to explore the touristy sites and restaurants of the city, and hang out with Matt sometimes too.

Writing this two years after the event, there are still a few notable memories.

Expensive meal and then night out. Marina bay sands. Formula 1 Raffles

The work

Sourced blockchain and AI based startups (seed to Series B) in Insuretech/Fintech/Mobility. Stroma Vision is a startup I sourced that uses image processing algorithms to track driver fatigue and improve safety.

Researched and produced investment reports. Interviewed CEO of company that uses AI models to quantify financial risk of extreme weather. Report included: product, business plan, team, market growth, competition, comparable EV/EBITDA ratios.

Chosen by Managing Director to attend Plug and Play Selection Day. Used business Mandarin to network with and entertain senior managers from Asian insurance companies, banks, venture capitalists and entrepreneurs.
